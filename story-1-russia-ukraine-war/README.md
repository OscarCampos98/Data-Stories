# 4 Years of War — What the Data Actually Shows

This data story analyzes the Russia-Ukraine war through public data, reports, and visual evidence to understand the scale of the conflict beyond daily battlefield updates.

The goal is to build an article-style analysis that explains the war through several connected dimensions: human losses, territorial change, economic damage, refugee displacement, foreign involvement, and the growing internationalization of the conflict.

Rather than focusing only on who controls which town or front line, this project asks a broader question:

> After years of war, what has each side actually paid — in lives, land, money, infrastructure, and global consequences?

## Story Focus

This analysis will explore the war through the following sections:

### 1. Human Cost

This section will examine casualty estimates over time, including Russian and Ukrainian military losses where reliable estimates are available.

The goal is not to present one exact number as absolute truth, but to compare credible estimates and show the scale, uncertainty, and trajectory of losses.

Possible chart ideas:

- Estimated casualties over time
- Monthly loss trends
- Comparison of casualty estimates by source
- Casualties compared with territorial gains

### 2. Territory vs. Cost

This section will compare Russian territorial gains against the human and material cost of the war.

The central idea is to examine whether Russia’s territorial gains justify the scale of losses, destruction, and long-term economic consequences.

Possible chart ideas:

- Territory controlled over time
- Square miles gained per year
- Estimated casualties per square mile gained
- Major offensive periods compared with territorial outcomes

### 3. Ukraine’s Economic and Infrastructure Damage

This section will analyze the economic collapse caused by the invasion, including GDP contraction, infrastructure destruction, energy system damage, poverty, and reconstruction needs.

The focus is on how the war has damaged Ukraine’s long-term economic capacity, even beyond the battlefield.

Possible chart ideas:

- Ukraine GDP before and after invasion
- Estimated reconstruction cost over time
- Energy infrastructure capacity loss
- Poverty rate changes
- Sector-level damage estimates

### 4. Refugee Crisis and Displacement

This section will examine the movement of Ukrainian refugees and internally displaced people.

The analysis will focus on where people went, how long displacement has lasted, how much host countries have spent, and whether refugees intend to return.

Possible chart ideas:

- Ukrainian refugees by host country
- Refugee flows over time
- EU host-country spending estimates
- Survey results on return intentions
- Internally displaced people vs. refugees abroad

### 5. The Globalization of the War

This section will explore how the war expanded beyond Russia and Ukraine through foreign fighters, state support, economic incentives, coercion, and military supply chains.

The analysis will look at different categories of foreign involvement, including:

- Ukrainian foreign volunteers
- Colombian fighters on Ukraine’s side
- Western ideological volunteers
- Technical and drone volunteers
- North Korean troops and artillery support to Russia
- Indian and African fighters reportedly recruited, trafficked, or coerced into Russian service
- State-to-state military exchanges and battlefield support

The Colombian case may be included as a side note because of the reported scale of Colombian participation on Ukraine’s side.

Possible chart ideas:

- Foreign fighters by reported country or region
- Categories of foreign fighter motivation
- North Korean military support timeline
- Reported artillery supply contribution by source
- Country involvement map

## Main Research Question

This project is built around the question:

> What does the data reveal about the real cost, scale, and international consequences of the Russia-Ukraine war after four years?

## Supporting Questions

- How have casualty estimates changed over time?
- How much territory has Russia gained compared with the cost paid?
- How badly has Ukraine’s economy and infrastructure been damaged?
- How large and long-lasting is the refugee crisis?
- How has the war become globalized through foreign fighters, state support, and supply chains?
- What does this conflict show about modern war, attrition, and international dependence?

## Planned Data Sources

This project will use public datasets, reports, and open-source estimates, including:

- Institute for the Study of War
- Kiel Institute Ukraine Support Tracker
- UNHCR
- World Bank
- World Bank Ukraine Rapid Damage and Needs Assessment
- Oryx equipment loss data
- CSIS
- Russia Matters
- United Nations datasets
- Additional public reporting where needed
